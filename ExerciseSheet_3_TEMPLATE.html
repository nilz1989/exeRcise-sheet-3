library(tidyverse)

food_waste <- read_csv("Food_waste_in_the_European_Union_2010.csv")

food_waste_clean <- food_waste %>%
  filter(Entity != "European Union")

food_waste_long <- food_waste_clean %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = c(`Manufacturing (Europa (2010))`, 
                        `Households (Europa (2010))`, 
                        `Other (Europa (2010))`),
               names_to = "Sector", values_to = "Food_waste_tonnes")

food_waste_long <- food_waste_long %>%
  mutate(Sector = case_when(
    str_detect(Sector, "Manufacturing") ~ "Manufacturing",
    str_detect(Sector, "Households") ~ "Households",
    str_detect(Sector, "Other") ~ "Other"
  ))

top10_total <- food_waste_long %>% 
  group_by(Entity) %>%
  summarise(total_waste = sum(Food_waste_tonnes, na.rm = TRUE)) %>%
  slice_max(total_waste, n=10) %>%
  pull(Entity)

top10_overall_df <- food_waste_long %>%
  filter(Entity %in% top10_total)

General observations:

The United Kingdom is the top contributor to total food waste, with particularly high waist levels in the household sector.

Germany and France also report substantial food waste, but more balance distribution across all three sectors.

Countries like Poland, Italy, and the Netherlands show especially high waisted in the manufacturing sector.

Spain stands out for having a relatively large contribution from the other category, compared to most other countries.

Belgium, Romania, and Sweden round out the top 10 with overall lower waist levels, but still notable.

Sectoral trends:

Households are the largest source of food waste across most countries, particularly in the UK, Germany, and France.

The manufacturing sector rank second overall, but leads in countries like Poland, the Netherlands and Italy.

The other category typically contributes the least though countries like Spain and the UK show notable values here as well.

Key takeaways:

Household behavior plays a dominant role in food waste generation across Europe
